A Short History of Scuba Diving

Humans explored the underwater world long before Scuba was ever invented. Since the start of time, people collected pearls or seafood to eat, from the ocean floor. They used heavy rocks as weights and held their breath, or had air supplied from the surface with makeshift snorkels.

By the 1600s new inventions had emerged to be used for scientific discovery and wreck salvage. One such was the Diving Bell, where the divers entire body was inside a giant bell, with air pumped into it. In the 1800s the Diving Helmet was created, which allowed the diver more freedom as only the head was encased in the helmet.

Many attempts were made in the 1800s to create a self contained system, in that the diver would carry their own air supply, and not have to be supplied from the surface.

Jacques Cousteau

Known as the "father of scuba diving" Jacques Cousteau invented the first Scuba Regulator, that could supply the diver with air on demand, and was self contained. He called it the “Aqualung”. Jacques Cousteau set off on his boat, The Calypso, on a decades long underwater exploration adventure, documenting it all. His documentaries and books were hugely popular. Thanks to this Scuba diving went from being a purely military and commercial activity to a recreational sport, for anyone to enjoy.

The sea, once it casts its spell, holds one in its net of wonder forever.

Jacques Cousteau, Life and Death in a Cora

Beginner

Learning to dive is a three part process:

  1. Theory
  2. Pool skills
  3. Open Water Dives

Theory

The first thing Scuba divers learn is how pressure at depth affects us. The following table illustrates how the deeper we go the more pressure is exerted and how volume increases while density decreases.

Pressure Volume Density Table
Depth Pressure Volume Density
0 m 1 ata/bar* 1 1
10 m 2 ata/bar x 2 1/2
20 m 3 ata/bar x 3 1/3
30 m 4 ata/bar x 4 1/4
* An ata/bar = atmosphere

At depth the weight of the water above us creates pressure on our bodies. Because our bodies are made of water we do not feel that weight. But we have air-spaces that do.

The three main air-spaces:

  • Ear and Sinus
  • Mask
  • Lungs

The deeper we go the more the air in these spaces compresses, which can cause pain if we don’t equalize (add air to) the air spaces

To equalize the ear and sinus we simply pinch our nose and blow against it.

To equalise the mask we blow a little air in to it through our nose.

To equalise the lungs we just continuously breath.

As illustrated in the table above, the volume of air expands as it gets shallower. This is why we never hold our breath when diving on Scuba, as with-out anywhere for the air to go our lungs will over expand and ..burst!

It is very important to get full training before Scuba diving!!

Pool Skills

For the pool skills portion of the beginner course, you will learn approximately 25 practical techniques that, together, make you a capable diver. Two of the most basic but most important of these skills are Mask Removal and and Regulator Recovery.

Mask Removal

To remove and replace the mask underwater

All these skills are initially done kneeling on the bottom of the pool for more control. To begin you will flood the mask to avoid suction. Gently pull the mask up and bring round to the front of your body. It’s very important to not let go as you cannot see to find it! The pool skills are practicing for real life and we would not want to lose our mask on a dive.

To put it back on you find the nose pocket and place that on your nose (you can also skip this step and place it upside down, which is very funny for the instructor). Slide the strap into place at the crown of your head and make sure it is not twisted. Then you clear the water from inside the mask by tilting your head back and blowing out your nose.

Regulator Recovery

To recover the regulator if it drops from the mouth

Again you will be kneeling on the bottom of the pool. Take a deep breath in and take out the regulator, dropping it to the right. Breath out continuously (remember to never hold your breath). With your right hand sweep back to your tank and up and round to the front. The regulator hose will be on your right arm. Place it back in to your mouth (right way up) and blow out to clear the water.

How will speak during our dive?

We cannot talk underwater but we must be able to communicate with our dive buddies. We use hand signals for this. The most used hand signal is the OK signal. Touch your index finger and thumb together to make an o, with the other fingers outstretched. Buoyancy

Buoyancy Control

Once you have mastered the equipment and the basic skills a very important technique to learn is buoyancy control. This allows you to go where you want in the water. Without controlling our buoyancy we could crash to the bottom or float to the surface. If we are too heavy and touch the bottom we risk injuring ourselves on sharp coral or disturbing marine life. If we float up we risk getting Decompression Sickness ("the bends"), or getting hit by a passing boat!

We mostly use our breathing to control our buoyancy. When we breath out and empty our lungs of air we will go down. When we inhale and fill our lungs we will go up, just like emptying or filling a balloon underwater.

Navigation

Underwater Navigation

Navigation underwater is very different to navigation on land. Even the clearest water and simplest dive site layout can prove a challenge to divers who haven’t honed their underwater navigation skills.

There are two main ways to navigate on our dives, natural navigation and compass navigation.

Natural Navigation

A common way to use natural navigation is to follow the edge of a reef or large landmark on your way out and when you turn around, just follow it back. Another way is to use slopes and shadows from sunlight or wave direction. These are of course only possible if you have a clear dive site with landmarks etc.

Compass Navigation

The more difficult but more precise way to navigate underwater is using a compass. Compass Navigation takes some practice on land and underwater before relying on it for your dive. With simple compass navigation we set our compass for the direction we are heading and watch that we don’t veer off course. Then when we want to return, we turn 180 degrees to swim back the way we came.

If we don’t want to swim in a straight line and back then we can make more complex shapes such as squares and triangles also using a compass. For this we either decide our path before the dive or use a slate underwater to write down how far we went before each turn, and how many degrees we turned.

The most important tip when using compass navigation is to trust your compass!!

What does SCUBA stand for?

Self Contained Underwater Breathing Apparatus.

What is bottom time?

The time we spend on our dive, that is not ascending and descending.

Why do divers roll back?

Because if they rolled forward they would go into the boat.
